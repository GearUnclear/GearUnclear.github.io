**Flood Safety** (Snohomish County) When a flood threatens: - Keep informed. Pay attention to safety messages from official sources such as the National Weather Service and local government. - Move to higher ground. Don't camp in flood prone areas. - Get out of areas subject to flooding: dips, low spots, floodplains, riverbanks, etc. - Stay out of areas with lots of trees during wind and heavy rain or high water. - Flooding season in Snohomish County typically begins in October and lasts into spring. During a flood: - Obey evacuation orders. - Avoid areas subject to sudden flooding. - Do not
show full message walk, swim or drive through flood waters. Turn Around, Don't Drown! - Just six inches of moving water can knock you down; one foot can sweep your vehicle away. Stay Out of Flooded Areas: - If you notice even a slight rise in water level, seek higher ground immediately. - If trapped by moving water, move to the highest possible point and call 911. On behalf of Brice Montgomery, Interim CSD Director: Replacement of Food Benefits Due to Household Misfortune or Disaster

Aug 06, 2024 · Denise Hollenbeck may be aging

Households that suffer a loss of food purchased with food benefits due to a household disaster or misfortune can contact the department for replacement of the loss, for up to one month's benefits. A household misfortune or disaster could be a fire, flood, Public Safety Power Shutoff or other power outage lasting four or more hours. How to claim a loss Households may report the loss of food purchased with food benefits, due to a household disaster or misfortune, verbally or in writing. The report must be made within 10 days of the loss. Additionally, federal rules and WAC 388-412-0040 require
show full message a signed statement that attests to the loss must be received from the household within 10 days of the date the loss was reported. Benefits cannot be replaced prior to timely receipt of the signed statement. The signed statement must include all of the following: - The head of household's signature - The date(s) the food purchased with Basic Food benefits was destroyed - The value of food lost in the disaster (purchased with food benefits) - The cause of loss or misfortune (e.g., a power outage for six hours) The statement could be a letter about the loss, a completed DSHS 12-008 DSHS Claim of Food Lost in a Household Disaster or by attestation to the loss with a telephonic signature. Staff will request additional details as needed to validate the claim, such as the power company and time of the outage, before processing the claim. Tips & Tidbits Newsletter — September 2025 Edition (Volume II, Issue 9) Published by Washington State Department of Social and Health Services (DSHS), Local Planning Areas (LPA) Partnerships Contact: Marcy Jordan, Local Planning Areas Partnerships, Program Manager marcy.jordan@dshs.wa.gov | 253-778-2438 | DSHSESASWLPALiaison@dshs.wa.gov Theme: "Recovery Is Real" — Restoring Every Aspect of Life This theme, promoted by SAMHSA, emphasizes the four key dimensions of recovery: health, home, community, and purpose. Recovery is a comprehensive process encompassing various aspects of an
show full message individual's life. WORDS MATTER: Preferred Language for Talking About Addiction Addiction is a chronic but treatable medical condition. Many people still talk about addiction in ways that are stigmatizing—using words that can portray someone with a substance use disorder (SUD) in a shameful or negative way, which may prevent them from seeking treatment. With simple changes in language, harmful stigma and negativity around SUD can be reduced or avoided. Stigma & Addiction What is stigma? Stigma is discrimination against an identifiable group of people, a place, or a nation. Stigma about people with substance use disorders might include inaccurate or unfounded thoughts like they are dangerous, incapable of managing treatment, or at fault for their condition. Where does stigma come from? Stigma around addiction may come from old and inaccurate ideas, or fear of things that are different or misunderstood. Today, we know that addiction is a chronic, treatable medical condition and that people can recover and continue to lead healthy lives. How does stigma affect people with SUD? Feeling stigmatized can make people with SUD less willing to seek treatment. Negative stereotypes can make others feel pity, fear, and even anger. How can we make a change? - Use words that aren't stigmatizing when talking to or about people with SUD. - Use person-first language, which focuses on the person—not their illness. For example, "person with a substance use disorder" has a neutral tone and separates the person from his or her disorder. - Let people choose how they are described. If unsure what words to use, ask. Addiction & the Brain Addiction is a chronic disease characterized by drug seeking and use that is compulsive, or difficult to control, despite harmful consequences. Repeated drug use can lead to brain changes that challenge self-control and interfere with the ability to resist intense urges to take drugs. These brain changes can be persistent, which is why drug addiction is considered a "relapsing" disease. Most drugs affect the brain's "reward circuit," causing euphoria and flooding it with dopamine. Long-term use causes changes in brain chemical systems affecting: learning, judgment, decision-making, stress, memory, and behavior. Resource: NIH video — "Why are Drugs so Hard to Quit?" The Washington State Department of Revenue launched the fourth year of the Working Families Tax Credit (WFTC) on Feb. 1, 2026. This annual state tax credit is available to low-to-moderate income individuals and families in Washington. Participants can receive up to $1,330 this year if they are generally eligible for the federal Earned Income Tax Credit and are a resident of Washington. On behalf of Brice Montgomery, Interim CSD Director: Replacement of Stolen Benefits is Ending

Dec 30, 2024 · Denise Hollenbeck may be aging

STATE OF WASHINGTON DEPARTMENT OF SOCIAL AND HEALTH SERVICES Community Services Division — Economic Services Administration Post Office Box 45440, Olympia WA 98504-5440 Dec. 26, 2024 Congress passed the Continuing Appropriations and Extensions Act, 2025, on Sept. 26, 2024, which extended the replacements authorized in the Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2023. The act required states to replace food benefits stolen between Oct. 1, 2022, and Dec. 20, 2024, via card skimming, card cloning and other similar, fraudulent methods. As of Dec. 26, 2024, Congress did not extend the date to replace
show full message these benefits. Based on current guidance, food benefits stolen on or after Dec. 21, 2024, cannot be replaced. Cash replacements will also end, as rules authorized under ESSB 5950 to replace Temporary Assistance for Needy Families, State Family Assistance, Pregnant Women's Assistance, Refugee Cash Assistance and Aged, Blind or Disabled cash benefits stolen July 1, 2024, or later mirror the end date for food replacements. The department will deny any claims for benefits that are stolen on or after Dec. 21, 2024. This change does not affect claims for benefits stolen on or before Dec. 20, 2024; claims will continue to be processed if reported within 30 days of discovery, even if the date of discovery is after Dec. 20, 2024. For questions, contact Sharon Hill-Laguerre, Basic Food Policy administrator: sharon.hill-laguerre@dshs.wa.gov
